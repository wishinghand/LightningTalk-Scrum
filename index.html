What is Agile Development?

Agile isn't just a set of ceremonies. It's a cultural and technical philosophy.

There are 7 main steps:

1. Discovery

2. Requirements Gathering

3. Analysis and Design

4. Implementation

5. Testing

6. Deployment

7. Maintenance

1. Discovery

What does the customer/client want?

2. Requirements Gathering

What will we need to accomplish it?

3. Analysis and Design

Writing of User Stories

4. Implementation

Writing the actual code

5. Testing

Making sure it's as bug free as possible

6. Deployment

Making it public and available to users or client

7. Maintenance

Fixing it when it breaks, making it last

There are 4 ceremonies:

1. Sprint Planning

2. Daily Standup

3. Sprint Demo

4. Sprint Retrospective

1. Sprint Planning

What does the customer/client want?

Ensuring the right size of tasks

How long the sprint should last (this is unchangeable once committed)

What the defintion of "done" is

What is a Sprint?

It's a fixed length iteration of a release backlog

How long does it last?

30 days max (limits risk to one month of cost)

How is it measured?

Each User Story is given a time estimate of 1, 2, 4, 8 hours or 2, 3, 5, 10 days

A burndown chart compares the estimated time left to the accomplished tasks and predicts if the project will be on time

2. Daily Standup

15 minutes max

Each member recites:

  • What they did yesterday
  • What they will do today
  • What obstacles are in the way of accomplishing today's goal

3. Sprint Demo

Showing the customer what your teams has accomplished in the sprint

Decisions made during the sprint

Project metrics (test coverage)

How it fits the defintion of "done"

4. Sprint Retrospective

Internal meeting for the team to review what they've done well

...And what they could improve on.

There are 3 roles:

1. Product Owner

2. Scrum Master

3. Development Team

1. Product Owner

Understand requirements

Writes User Stories

Build/manage backlog

Defines priorities of user stories in backlog

Decide when to ship

2. Scrum Master

Coach the team

Guide the process of development

Stay in touch with product owner

Stops scope change/creep from happening

Handle resources needed for team to finish sprint

3. Development Team

3-9 members, usually 5-7

No sub teams

They cross train to cover all roles

What are the pros and cons?

Pros

Large projects are divided into easily manageable sprints

Commits are coded and tested during the sprint review

Short sprints enable changes based on feedback a lot more easily

Progress is more clearly felt by Product Owners and Team Members

Cons

The chances of project failure are high if individuals aren't very committed or cooperative

The methodology can be hard to adopt on large teams

Can be susceptible to scope creep, less predictable outcomes

Early iterations are suboptimal (customer feedback helps fix this as iterations go on)
